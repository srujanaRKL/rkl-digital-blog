SMS Marketing for Small Business: A Practical Guide

By the RKL Digital Team · Last updated June 17, 2026 · 8 min read

Quick Answer

SMS marketing is sending short, opt-in text messages — offers, reminders, and updates — to customers who agreed to receive them, and it gets read almost instantly with open rates around 98%. For small businesses, it's the fastest way to reach customers for time-sensitive messages. The keys are getting proper consent, keeping it short, and reserving texts for things that genuinely deserve a buzz in someone's pocket.

Nothing else gets read like a text. The average email might sit unopened for hours; the average text is read within three minutes. That immediacy makes SMS incredibly powerful — and incredibly easy to overuse. Get it right and it's the most direct line you have to a customer. Get it wrong and people opt out fast.

This guide covers how small businesses use SMS the right way: staying compliant, what to send, real examples, and how to combine texting with your email marketing so the two channels reinforce each other instead of overwhelming people.

The golden rule: get consent first

SMS is heavily regulated in the U.S. under the TCPA. Before you text anyone, you need clear, documented opt-in consent — usually a checkbox or a keyword opt-in ("Text JOIN to 55555"). Every message must identify your business and include an easy opt-out ("Reply STOP to unsubscribe").

This isn't just legal box-checking. Permission-based texting is what keeps your messages welcome instead of annoying. A compliant SMS platform handles consent records, opt-outs, and message formatting for you, so you don't have to track it manually.

The rule of thumb: if it's short, urgent, and useful, text it. If it's longer or educational, email it. Many small businesses run both through one marketing automation system so the right channel fires automatically.

The big insight: SMS punishes over-sending faster than any other channel. Because a text feels personal, two well-timed messages a month earn loyalty, while two a week earn opt-outs. Treat that inbox like a privilege, not a billboard.

SMS examples that work

Keep texts under 160 characters when you can, lead with the value, and include one clear action:

  • Offer: "Maple St. Diner: 20% off all week to our text crew. Show this text. Reply STOP to opt out."
  • Reminder: "Hi Sam, reminder: your appt is tomorrow at 2pm. Reply C to confirm or R to reschedule."
  • Back-in-stock: "Good news — the item on your wishlist is back. Grab it here: [link]. Reply STOP to opt out."
  • Win-back: "We miss you! Here's 15% off your next order, just for today: [link]."

Notice each one is specific, friendly, and respects the customer's time. That tone is what keeps your opt-out rate low.

Make SMS part of a bigger system

SMS works best as one piece of a connected funnel, not a standalone blast. Capture opt-ins through your website, confirm with a welcome text, then let automation send reminders and offers at the right moments. When you tie SMS into your lead nurturing funnel, you reach people on whichever channel they respond to fastest.

Frequently asked questions

What is SMS marketing?

Sending promotional or transactional text messages to customers who opted in — offers, appointment reminders, order updates, and alerts. It's read almost immediately, with open rates around 98%.

Is SMS marketing legal for small business?

Yes, but it's regulated. Under the TCPA you must get clear opt-in consent before texting, identify your business, and include an easy opt-out in every message. A compliant platform keeps you on the right side of the rules.

What's a good SMS open rate?

Around 98%, with most texts read within three minutes. That far exceeds email, making SMS ideal for time-sensitive messages like flash sales, reminders, and order updates.

How often should I text my customers?

Keep it light — about two to four texts a month for most small businesses. Texts feel personal, so over-sending causes fast opt-outs. Reserve SMS for genuinely useful or urgent messages.

Should I use SMS or email marketing?

Use both — they do different jobs. Email suits longer content and nurturing; SMS suits urgent, short messages that need to be seen now. The strongest setups combine and automate the two together.
