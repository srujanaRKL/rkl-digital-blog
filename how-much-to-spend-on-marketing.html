How Much Should a Small Business Spend on Marketing?

By the RKL Digital Team · Last updated June 17, 2026 · 8 min read

Quick Answer

Most established small businesses should spend 7–10% of gross revenue on marketing, and 12–20% if they're in aggressive growth mode or launching something new. Brand-new businesses without revenue history usually set a fixed monthly amount they can sustain for 6–12 months, since most channels compound over time rather than pay off instantly. The right number is the one that lets you acquire customers profitably — measured by cost per customer, not gut feel.

"How much should I spend on marketing?" is one of the most common — and most misunderstood — questions small business owners ask. Spend too little and you never build momentum. Spend too much in the wrong place and you burn cash with nothing to show. The answer isn't a magic dollar figure; it's a framework you can apply to your own numbers.

This guide gives you the benchmarks, a channel-by-channel split, and a way to scale spend as you grow. It pairs with our broader small business digital marketing strategy guide, which covers which channels to choose in the first place.

The benchmark: percentage of revenue

The most common rule of thumb ties marketing spend to revenue. The U.S. Small Business Administration has long pointed to roughly 7–8% of revenue for businesses under $5 million a year with healthy margins. From there, adjust up or down based on three factors:

  • Growth stage. Defending a stable business needs less than aggressively taking market share.
  • Margins. A high-margin service business can reinvest more than a thin-margin retailer.
  • Competition. Crowded, high-cost markets demand more spend just to be seen.

How to split the budget across channels

Knowing the total is half the battle; the other half is allocation. A practical starting split for most small businesses looks like this — then you rebalance based on what's actually producing leads.

  1. 40–50% — Foundation (compounding). Your website, SEO, AI search visibility, and content. This is the asset that keeps working without paying per click. Anchor it with a strong SEO foundation.
  2. 30–40% — Paid acquisition (speed). Google and social ads that bring leads now while the foundation matures.
  3. 10–20% — Retention & tools. Email, automation, CRM, and the software that ties it together.

If your budget is genuinely tight, our guide to digital marketing on a small budget shows how to get traction with very little — without wasting a dollar.

The big insight: a marketing budget isn't a cost to minimize — it's an engine you tune. The goal is the lowest cost per acquired customer, not the lowest spend. A business spending $3,000 a month to win $30,000 in new revenue should be looking for ways to spend more, not less.

Spend by channel: where the money actually goes

It helps to see what specific channels cost in practice for a small business:

  • SEO & AI SEO: often the highest-ROI line over 12 months because you don't pay per click — but it needs consistent investment to compound.
  • Paid search: you control the dial; spend can be $500/month or $50,000/month, but leads stop the moment you turn it off.
  • Email & automation: very low cost, often the best return per dollar once you have a list to nurture.
  • Agency or specialist fees: commonly $1,000–$5,000/month for small businesses, with ad spend billed separately.

Deciding whether to keep marketing in-house or hire out is its own question — see in-house vs. agency vs. freelancer to weigh the trade-offs against your budget.

Prove the budget is working

Whatever you spend, the only way to know it's right is to measure return. Track cost per lead by channel, your close rate, average customer value, and ultimately revenue generated per dollar spent. Frequently asked questions

How much should a small business spend on marketing?

A widely used benchmark is 7–10% of gross revenue for established businesses, and 12–20% in aggressive growth mode or at launch. New businesses without revenue history pick a fixed monthly amount they can sustain for 6–12 months.

What percentage of revenue should go to marketing?

The SBA commonly cites 7–8% for businesses under $5M a year with healthy margins. Lower-margin businesses spend less; high-growth or competitive markets spend more. The right number lets you acquire customers profitably and sustainably.

How should I split my marketing budget across channels?

A practical start is 40–50% to foundational channels like SEO, website, and content, 30–40% to paid ads for speed, and 10–20% to email, automation, and tools. Rebalance quarterly toward the cheapest qualified leads.

Is it better to spend on SEO or paid ads with a small budget?

Do both at a modest level: SEO builds compounding demand you don't pay per click for, while a small paid campaign brings leads while SEO matures. If you must choose one, match it to your timeline — ads are faster, SEO is cheaper long-term.

How much does it cost to hire a marketing agency?

Small business engagements commonly run about $1,000–$5,000/month depending on scope, with ad spend billed separately. The goal isn't the lowest price — it's the lowest cost per acquired customer.
