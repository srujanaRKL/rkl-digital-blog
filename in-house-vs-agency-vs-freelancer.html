In-House vs. Agency vs. Freelancer: What's Best for Your Budget?

By the RKL Digital Team · Last updated June 17, 2026 · 8 min read

Quick Answer

Hire a freelancer for a single specialized task on a tight budget, an agency when you need multiple skills and consistent execution across channels without managing employees, and go in-house once marketing is a daily core function you can fund with a full salary plus tools. For most small businesses, an agency offers the best value: you get a team of specialists — strategy, SEO, ads, design — for less than the cost of one salaried generalist, and far more coverage than a single freelancer.

Once you've decided marketing deserves real investment, the next question is who actually does the work. The three options — in-house employee, agency, or freelancer — each have a place. Choosing wrong wastes money and momentum, so it's worth matching the model to your stage and budget on purpose.

This guide breaks down cost, speed, and risk for each, then helps you decide. It pairs with our guide on how much to spend on marketing — because the right hiring model depends heavily on the budget you've set.

When in-house makes sense

An in-house hire gives you the most control and someone fully dedicated to your brand. But you're paying a full salary plus benefits, software, and the time it takes to manage and train them — and you get one person's skill set, not a whole team's. In-house tends to pay off once marketing is a daily, central function and your volume justifies a dedicated role. Many small businesses simply aren't there yet.

When a freelancer makes sense

Freelancers are perfect for a defined, specialized job: a logo, a batch of blog posts, a single ad campaign. They're affordable and fast to engage. The catch is they cover one skill and become a single point of failure — if your freelancer is booked or disappears, your marketing stalls. They rarely own end-to-end strategy, so you're left coordinating the pieces yourself.

When an agency makes sense

An agency bundles strategy and multiple specialists under one roof, with accountability for results. For roughly the cost of a part-time salary, you get SEO, paid ads, content, design, and analytics working together. This is why agencies are often the best value for small businesses: the breadth of a team without the overhead of employees. The hybrid model most growing businesses land on

You don't have to pick just one. A common and effective setup combines all three:

  • A small in-house presence — an owner or coordinator who sets direction and approves work.
  • An agency — for strategy and core channels like SEO, AI search, and paid ads that need consistent, expert execution. RKL Digital fills this role with SEO services and full-funnel marketing.
  • Freelancers — for occasional specialized work like video editing or a one-off design.

This gives you control where it matters, breadth where you need it, and flexibility for the extras.

How to decide for your business

Run through these questions:

  1. Is this one project or an ongoing program? One project → freelancer. Ongoing → agency or in-house.
  2. How many skills does it require? One → freelancer. Several → agency.
  3. Can you afford and manage a full salary? Yes, and marketing is central → consider in-house. Not yet → agency.
  4. Do you have time to coordinate the pieces? No → an agency removes that burden.

Frequently asked questions

Should a small business hire in-house, an agency, or a freelancer for marketing?

It depends on budget and scope. A freelancer suits a single specialized task on a tight budget. An agency suits multiple skills and consistent multi-channel execution without managing employees. In-house fits once marketing is a daily core function you can fund with a full salary and tools.

Is an agency or a freelancer cheaper?

A single freelancer usually has a lower monthly rate but covers one skill. An agency costs more but bundles strategy, multiple specialists, and accountability. The cheaper option per hour can cost more per result if it leaves gaps.

How much does an in-house marketer cost vs an agency?

A full-time small-business marketer typically costs $50,000–$80,000/year in salary plus benefits, tools, and management. A small-business agency engagement commonly runs $1,000–$5,000/month — a team of specialists for less than one salaried generalist.

What are the downsides of hiring a freelancer?

Freelancers cover one skill, can be a single point of failure if unavailable, and rarely own end-to-end strategy. They're great for a defined project but harder to rely on for a full, always-on program.

Can I mix in-house, agency, and freelancers?

Yes. A common model is a small in-house owner or coordinator setting direction, an agency for strategy and core channels like SEO and ads, and freelancers for occasional specialized work such as video or design.
