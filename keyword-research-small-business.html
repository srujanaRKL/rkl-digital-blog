How to Do Keyword Research for a Small Business

By the RKL Digital Team · Last updated June 17, 2026 · 9 min read

Quick Answer

Keyword research is the process of discovering the exact words and questions your customers type or speak when searching for what you offer — so you can build pages that match. For a small business, the winning approach is to list the problems you solve, expand that list with free tools and Google's own suggestions, then sort the results by intent and difficulty. Target specific, lower-competition "long-tail" phrases first; they're easier to rank for and bring in customers who are ready to buy.

Most small businesses guess at what their customers search for — and guess wrong. They optimize for industry jargon while their actual customers use plain, everyday language. Keyword research closes that gap. It hands you the real phrases people use, so every page you build answers a question someone is already asking.

This guide shows you how to do keyword research without expensive tools or an SEO degree. It's the foundation of every successful campaign and a core part of our small business SEO guide.

Why keyword research matters for small businesses

You have limited time and budget, so you can't afford to create content nobody searches for. Keyword research tells you which topics have demand, how hard they are to rank for, and what customers want when they search. It turns "I think we should write about X" into "people search this 1,200 times a month and our competitors barely cover it."

It also reveals the language of your market. The phrase a customer types — "why is my AC blowing warm air" — is often very different from how you'd describe the service. Writing in their words is what gets you found, and increasingly, what gets you cited by AI assistants too.

The 5 steps of keyword research

Step 1: Brainstorm seed topics

Start with a simple list of your products, services, and the problems you solve. If you run an HVAC company, seeds might be "AC repair," "furnace installation," "thermostat not working." Think like a customer, not a marketer.

Step 2: Expand your list

Take each seed and grow it using free sources:

  • Google autocomplete — start typing and note the suggestions.
  • "People also ask" and "Related searches" on the results page.
  • Google Keyword Planner — free with a Google Ads account, shows volume and competition.
  • Competitor pages — see which terms they target in headings and titles.

Step 3: Sort by search intent

Every keyword has a goal behind it. Match your page type to that goal, or you won't rank no matter how good the content is.

Step 4: Judge difficulty and volume

A keyword's value depends on the balance of how many people search it (volume) and how hard it is to rank (difficulty). As a small business, avoid broad head terms like "insurance" and favor specific long-tail phrases. They convert better and are realistic to rank for. We explain why being specific pays off in our guide to blog content that ranks.

Step 5: Map keywords to pages

Group related keywords into one topic per page — don't create a separate page for every tiny variation. One strong page targeting a primary keyword plus several close variants outperforms ten thin pages. Then build each page properly using our on-page SEO checklist.

The big insight: Long-tail keywords are a small business's biggest advantage. You'll rarely beat a national brand for "running shoes," but you can absolutely own "wide-toe trail running shoes for flat feet" — and that searcher is ready to buy.

Keyword research for AI search

Search behavior is shifting toward full questions, both in voice search and in AI assistants like ChatGPT and Perplexity. That makes question-based and conversational keywords more valuable than ever. Pay close attention to "how," "why," "what's the best," and "near me" phrasings — these mirror exactly how people prompt AI tools, and answering them clearly is what earns citations.

Free vs. paid keyword tools

  • Free: Google Keyword Planner, Google Trends, autocomplete, "People also ask," and AnswerThePublic give you plenty to start.
  • Paid: Tools like Ahrefs, Semrush, and SE Ranking add precise volume, difficulty scores, and competitor gap analysis — useful once you're scaling.

You don't need paid tools to begin. Start free, prove the wins, then invest as you grow.

Common keyword research mistakes

  • Chasing volume over intent. A high-volume keyword that doesn't match your offer brings traffic that never buys.
  • Targeting terms that are too competitive. Ranking for "lawyer" is a multi-year battle; "DUI lawyer in Waukesha" is winnable.
  • Stuffing keywords unnaturally. Write for humans first; modern search and AI penalize awkward repetition.
  • Ignoring questions. Question keywords are gold for both featured snippets and AI answers.

Frequently asked questions

What is keyword research?

Keyword research is the process of finding the words and phrases people type or speak into search engines and AI assistants when looking for what you offer. It tells you what content to create, which pages to build, and how customers actually describe your products and services.

How do I find keywords for my small business?

List the products, services, and problems you solve, then expand that list using Google autocomplete, the "People also ask" box, a free tool like Google Keyword Planner, and competitor pages. Group the results by topic and intent before choosing which to target.

What are long-tail keywords?

Long-tail keywords are longer, more specific phrases like "emergency furnace repair in Brookfield" rather than broad terms like "furnace repair." They have lower search volume but much higher intent and are far easier for a small business to rank for.

What is search intent?

Search intent is the goal behind a search. The four main types are informational, navigational, commercial, and transactional. Matching your content to the intent is essential to rank and convert.

How many keywords should a small business target?

Focus on one primary keyword and a handful of related terms per page rather than chasing dozens at once. A typical small business site might build out 10 to 30 well-targeted pages over time, each owning a clear topic.
