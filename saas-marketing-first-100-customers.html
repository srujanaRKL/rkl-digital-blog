SaaS Marketing 101: Getting Your First 100 Customers

By the RKL Digital Team · Last updated June 17, 2026 · 10 min read

Quick Answer

Early SaaS startups get their first 100 customers through founder-led sales, ruthless focus on one painful problem, content and SEO that capture high-intent search, and direct outreach in the communities where their ideal users already gather — not through big ad budgets. The first 100 customers are won by talking to people and being genuinely findable, then converting them with a clear free trial or demo and email follow-up. Paid ads come later, once your messaging and funnel are proven.

The marketing that gets a SaaS company from 0 to 100 customers looks almost nothing like the marketing that scales it to 10,000. Early on, you don't have a brand, a budget, or proven messaging — you have a product and a problem to validate. That changes the playbook entirely.

This guide lays out the channels and sequence that actually work for the first 100 customers in 2026. It builds on the fundamentals in our digital marketing strategy guide and goes deep on what's specific to SaaS.

Step 1: Narrow your focus until it hurts

The first mistake early SaaS founders make is talking to everyone. The fix is to pick one specific customer with one painful, expensive problem and aim everything at them. A sharp message to a narrow audience converts far better than a broad message to a wide one — and it makes every channel below dramatically easier.

When you can name exactly who you help and the precise pain you remove, your homepage, your outreach, and your content all write themselves.

Step 2: Start with founder-led sales

For the first 100 customers, nothing beats the founder personally selling. Direct conversations do three things at once: they close early customers, they reveal the exact words buyers use (which become your marketing copy), and they expose what your product is missing. Reach out one-to-one to people who clearly have the problem, get them on calls, and learn relentlessly.

This phase isn't "unscalable busywork" — it's how you discover the messaging and positioning that the scalable channels will later amplify.

Step 3: Build content and SEO that capture intent

SaaS buyers research heavily before they buy, which makes content and search the highest-leverage organic channel. Write content that answers the exact questions your buyer searches — comparisons, how-to guides, and "best tool for X" pieces — and you capture prospects at the moment of high intent. Our content marketing playbook lays out the full system.

In 2026 this increasingly means being cited by AI assistants too. When a buyer asks ChatGPT or Perplexity "what's the best tool for [problem]," you want to be in that answer. Clear, direct, well-structured content is what gets pulled in — the same approach we cover across our AI-first work.

The big insight: Your first 100 customers are a learning goal as much as a revenue goal. Every conversation and signup teaches you which channel, message, and customer to double down on. Optimize for insight now, and the path to the next 1,000 becomes obvious.

Step 4: Go where your users already gather

Before you have an audience, borrow other people's. Show up genuinely in the communities, forums, newsletters, and groups where your ideal customers already spend time. Answer questions, share what you've learned, and let your product come up naturally. Targeted partnerships and integrations with tools your customers already use can also open a warm channel to exactly the right people.

Step 5: Build a simple funnel that converts

Once you're attracting the right visitors, you need a funnel that turns them into paying users. Early on, keep it simple and focused on getting people to value fast.

The two stages startups most often neglect are activation and follow-up. A great trial experience that gets users to value quickly, paired with a strong email sequence, converts far more signups to paid. Our lead nurturing funnel guide shows how to build that follow-up so trials don't go cold.

Step 6: Hold off on paid ads (for now)

It's tempting to buy your way to traction, but paid ads usually burn cash before you've nailed your messaging and funnel. The disciplined move is to validate positioning with founder-led sales and organic channels first, prove that trials convert to paid, and only then pour paid spend on the channels and messages you've confirmed work. Paid is an amplifier, not a substitute for product-market fit.

Putting it together

The first 100 SaaS customers come from a tight loop: focus on one painful problem, sell directly to learn the language, publish content that captures search and AI intent, show up where users gather, and convert them through a simple funnel with strong follow-up. Do this well and you'll exit the first 100 with something more valuable than revenue — a proven, repeatable engine you can scale. Frequently asked questions

How does a SaaS startup get its first 100 customers?

Through founder-led sales, narrow targeting of one painful problem, content and SEO that capture high-intent search, and direct outreach in the communities where ideal users gather. Paid ads come later — the first 100 are won through focus and conversation, not big budgets.

What is the best marketing channel for early-stage SaaS?

There's no single best channel, but founder-led sales plus content and SEO are the most reliable for the first 100. They're low-cost, compound over time, and reach buyers actively searching for a solution. The right mix depends on where your customers gather.

Should an early SaaS startup spend on paid ads?

Usually not for the first 100 customers. Until you have proven messaging and a converting funnel, paid ads tend to burn cash. Validate positioning with founder-led sales and organic channels first, then use paid ads to scale what works.

How important is content marketing for SaaS?

Very. SaaS buyers research heavily before buying, so content that answers their questions and ranks in search — and increasingly in AI assistants — captures high-intent prospects cheaply. It compounds, making it one of the highest-ROI channels for SaaS growth.

How do you build a SaaS marketing funnel?

Attract visitors with content and SEO, convert them with a clear free trial or demo, then nurture with email until they activate and pay. The keys are a focused offer, a fast path to value in-product, and email follow-up that moves trials to paid.
